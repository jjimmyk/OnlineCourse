First off, a SMART Goal must be specific. But, what does that really mean?

 

Success Criteria 2.0: Distinguish goals that are specific from those that are not.

Example:

Meet Susana, a 10th-grader in Miami, Florida.

After watching the Olympics with her family, Susana has decided that she wants to be an Olympic swimmer.

Learn by Doing

What is the most specific goal that Susana could set?

Note: Specificity is a spectrum. We want our goals to be as specific as possible. Whenever you can make your goal more precise, do it! This will help you measure and evaluate how close you are to your goal.

 

Success Criteria 2.1: Distinguish goals that are measurable from those that are not.

Making a goal specific is nice, but you also need to measure how close you are to meeting it.

Let's revisit Susana and make her goal specific and measurable.

Example:

Meet Susana, a 10th-grader in Miami, Florida.

After watching the Olympics with her family, Susana has decided that she wants to be an Olympic swimmer.

Learn by Doing

What is the most specific and measurable goal that Susana could set?

 

Making a goal measurable makes it more specific, too! There's a big difference between swimming one lap and twenty laps with the correct freestyle stroke.
